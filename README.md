# Clinical Data Invisibility in LMICs

## A Pilot for AI-Ready and Biosurveillance-Ready Health Data Infrastructure

## Overview

Modern AI systems, epidemiological forecasting tools, and biosurveillance platforms depend on structured, high-quality health datasets.

However, many high-burden, low-resource healthcare settings remain structurally underrepresented in these datasets—not due to lack of clinical activity, but because routine clinical information is not consistently transformed into structured, reusable data.

This project investigates the upstream infrastructure problem:

> How and why does routine clinical information fail to become usable data for public health surveillance and AI systems?


## Project Goal

This pilot explores whether a lightweight, workflow-aligned data capture approach can improve the visibility and usability of routine clinical information in healthcare facilities in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo.

The focus is not on building an electronic health record system or digital health platform.

Instead, the project studies the minimal infrastructure required for routine clinical care to generate structured data that can support:

* biosurveillance systems
* epidemiological monitoring
* public health decision-making
* AI model development and evaluation


## Key Research Questions

* Where does clinical information become lost or unstructured in routine care workflows?
* What information is already collected but not usable for secondary analysis?
* What operational constraints prevent structured data capture?
* What minimal intervention could improve data visibility without increasing clinical burden?

If implemented, the pilot will produce:

* Clinical data flow maps showing where information is lost
* Identification of structural barriers to data usability
* A lightweight structured data capture template
* A small anonymized dataset from real-world implementation
* A field report with operational recommendations


## Why This Matters for AI and Biosurveillance

AI systems and biosurveillance platforms rely on data that is often unevenly distributed across geographies and health systems.

This creates a structural blind spot:

* some populations are overrepresented in datasets
* others are effectively invisible to data-driven systems

This project focuses on the upstream layer of that problem:

> the transformation of routine clinical activity into structured, reusable data.

Improving this layer is a prerequisite for:

* more representative AI systems in healthcare
* stronger outbreak detection and monitoring systems
* more accurate epidemiological models
* better-informed global health decision-making

## Core Framing

This project investigates a neglected bottleneck in global health intelligence: the infrastructure required for routine clinical activity to become machine-readable data. By studying where clinical information is lost before it enters surveillance, forecasting, and AI pipelines, the project aims to identify scalable interventions that improve the visibility of underrepresented populations in data-driven systems.

The central hypothesis is that many populations remain effectively invisible to data-driven systems not because healthcare activity is absent, but because the infrastructure required to transform routine clinical information into structured, reusable data is missing or incomplete. Improving this layer is a prerequisite for more representative AI systems, stronger biosurveillance platforms, and more effective global health intelligence systems.
